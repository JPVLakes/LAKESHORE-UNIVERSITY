### Section B: Process & Decision-Making (Open Response)

**Q11.** When you receive a request that could legitimately be handled by two different departments (for example, a material quality complaint that involves both Compras and QPS), walk us through how you decide which department takes the lead and how you coordinate between them.

**Q12.** Describe a situation where you had to re-route a request that was initially sent to the wrong department. What signals told you it was misrouted, and how did you handle it without creating confusion or delays?

**Q13.** When multiple departments are competing for priority on the same house (for example, Scheduling wants to advance but Inspecao has a hold), how do you decide whose need takes precedence? What factors do you weigh?

**Q14.** Describe how you handle the situation when a department leader is unresponsive to a request that is blocking progress on a house. What is your escalation timeline and who do you involve at each step?

**Q15.** Walk us through a typical Monday morning. What do you check first, what information do you gather before the day starts, and how do you prioritize the day's coordination tasks?

**Q16.** When Bernardo or Leo gives a directive that changes the priority of an ongoing project, how do you cascade that change to all affected departments? Describe the communication chain you follow.

**Q17.** Explain how you handle a situation where a subcontractor failed an inspection three times on the same house. Who do you notify, in what order, and what information do you include?

**Q18.** How do you handle the transition when a house moves from one supervisor to another mid-build (for example, when Francisco hands off to Jaaziel)? What coordination is needed to ensure nothing falls through the cracks?

**Q19.** Describe how you manage information flow when a county has a different inspection requirement than what the team expected. For example, Citrus County requires a Structural Frame inspection that Marion County does not. How do you ensure the right teams are informed before it causes a delay?

**Q20.** When a client puts a hold on their project (for personal financial reasons, design changes, etc.), walk us through everything that needs to happen across departments to properly pause and eventually resume the project.

### Section C: Advanced Scenarios & Edge Cases (Open Response)

**Q21.** A house in Charlotte County is 160 days into its permit with no inspection activity. A hurricane just hit the area, and the sub who was scheduled for the next phase is now working disaster recovery jobs and unavailable for 3 weeks. The permit will expire in 20 days. Describe every step you would take, every department you would contact, and the priority order of your actions.

**Q22.** You discover that a "Checklist Done" was marked by a supervisor, triggering payment to a sub, but QPS later finds the work was substandard. The sub has already been paid. Walk us through the departments involved in resolving this, the difficult conversations that need to happen, and how you prevent it from happening again.

**Q23.** Two houses in the same community need the same specialized sub (the only one available for that trade in the area). Both houses are on tight timelines. The scheduling team is asking you to prioritize. What information do you gather from each department before making a recommendation, and what factors determine which house gets the sub first?

**Q24.** A new county (let's say Volusia) is being added to Lakeshore's operations. As the coordinator, describe everything that needs to happen across departments before the first house can break ground there. Include regulatory, operational, financial, and system-level preparations.

**Q25.** It is Friday at 4 PM. You receive three urgent requests simultaneously: (1) A failed Building Final inspection on a house scheduled for closing next Wednesday, (2) A permit about to expire on Monday with no inspection scheduled, (3) A sub threatening to walk off a job site over a payment dispute. In what order do you address these, who do you contact for each, and why?

**Q26.** You notice that the same type of inspection (let's say Rough Electric) has failed on 4 out of the last 6 houses in Marion County, but each house used a different electrical sub. What does this pattern tell you, who do you involve, and what systemic investigation would you recommend?

**Q27.** A department leader (let's say Scheduling) and another leader (let's say Supervisao) are in open disagreement about whether a phase is ready to advance. Scheduling says the checklist is done. Supervisao says the quality is not acceptable. How do you mediate this in real-time, who has final authority, and how do you document the resolution?

**Q28.** Describe the informal communication channels that actually run the day-to-day operations at Lakeshore. Beyond the official tools (BuilderTrend, Slack, email), how does information really flow between departments? Which phone calls, text messages, or hallway conversations are critical to keeping things moving?

**Q29.** When you look at the pipeline of active houses and see that 12 houses are all about to hit the Drywall phase in the same two-week window, what coordination problems do you anticipate? What preemptive actions do you take with Scheduling, Compras, Deposito, and the sub pool?

**Q30.** Describe the single most common coordination failure you see at Lakeshore -- the one that causes the most delays or cost overruns. What is it, why does it keep happening, and if you could change one thing about how departments communicate, what would it be?
