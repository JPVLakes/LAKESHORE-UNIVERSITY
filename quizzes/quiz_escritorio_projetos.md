### Section B: Process & Decision-Making (Open Response)

**Q11.** Walk through how you develop the initial design concept when a new custom client contacts Lakeshore. What questions do you ask in the first meeting? How much of the design do you produce before the pre-agreement is signed?

**Q14.** Describe how you decide on the standard specifications for a new spec home model — door sizes, ceiling heights, floor types, cabinet styles, etc. Who is involved in these decisions, and how do you balance cost targets with market appeal?

**Q15.** Explain the "allowance" system for custom home budgets. How do you decide which items get allowances vs. fixed pricing? What happens when a client picks a finish that exceeds their allowance after the contract is signed?

**Q17.** How do you handle it when a client wants a design element that you know will significantly increase construction cost or complexity? Describe your process for managing client expectations while keeping the project viable.

**Q18.** When Permits needs a floor plan for application and the design hasn't been finalized yet, what do you do? Is there a "good enough" version that Permits can use, or does it always have to be the final version?

### Section C: Advanced Scenarios & Edge Cases (Open Response)

**Q21.** A custom client is at the pre-agreement stage and has made 4 significant design changes in 2 weeks — they've changed the master bedroom layout, moved the kitchen island, switched from a hip roof to a gable roof, and added a covered porch. Each change requires re-work. At what point do you push back, and how do you structure the conversation about additional design fees?

**Q22.** Bernardo asks you to develop 3 new spec home models for Charlotte County expansion, targeting the $250K-$350K price range. Charlotte has different wind speed requirements (140-150 mph vs. 120 mph in Marion). Walk through your design approach — what do you change from existing Marion models, what structural elements are affected, and how does this impact the budget?

**Q23.** You've just learned that a floor plan you sent to Permits 2 weeks ago had an error — a window was dimensioned incorrectly. The permit has already been submitted to Marion County. Describe your response plan. Who do you notify first? How does this affect the permit timeline? What does the county require to correct this?

**Q24.** A custom client wants to replicate a design they saw on Pinterest — a modern farmhouse with specific materials and finishes that are not standard in the Florida market. How do you translate their vision into a buildable design? What compromises might be necessary, and how do you research availability and cost of non-standard materials?

**Q25.** You're working on spec home models and Samuel tells you the budget per square foot needs to drop by $15 across all new models to stay competitive. Where in the design can you make changes that reduce cost without reducing perceived quality or violating code? Walk through at least 5 specific design decisions you'd consider.

**Q26.** Describe the full lifecycle of a Design Book from creation to completion. How many people touch it? How does it evolve as the project moves from concept to construction? What's the biggest risk in the Design Book process?

**Q27.** A field supervisor calls to say the wall framing doesn't match the floor plan — there's a discrepancy of 6 inches in the master closet width. After investigation, you realize the engineering drawings added a shear wall that wasn't in your original design. How do you resolve this? Who has authority to decide which dimension wins?

**Q28.** You have two custom home projects running simultaneously: one client is highly engaged and changes specifications weekly, while the other is absent and unresponsive to requests for decisions on finishes. Describe your strategy for managing both. What deadlines do you enforce, and what happens to construction scheduling when client decisions are delayed?

**Q29.** If you could design a "product library" as Bernardo has suggested — pre-defined models, elevation options, and floor plan bases that can be adapted rather than reinvented — what would the first version look like? What elements would you standardize, what would remain customizable, and how would this change the workflow?

**Q30.** Think about the information that flows out of your department to ALL other departments (Budget, Permits, Field, Client Relations, Compras, Scheduling). What information is most often missing, delayed, or misunderstood when it leaves your team? What's the single biggest communication breakdown, and what would fix it?
