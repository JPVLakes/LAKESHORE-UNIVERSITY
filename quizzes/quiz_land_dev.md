# Land Development -- Knowledge Assessment
## Lakeshore Homes Professional Development Program

*This assessment helps us understand expertise across our team and identify areas for professional growth. Your detailed responses will help us build stronger land acquisition and site development processes.*

---

**Q2.** In Lake County, what geological risk requires a geotechnical investigation before development?

- C) Earthquakes
- D) Soil contamination

**Q3.** In Marion County, what size oak tree requires a removal permit?
- A) Any oak tree
- B) Oaks over 6 inches DBH
- C) Oaks over 12 inches DBH

**Q4.** What does the compact test verify during site preparation?

**Q5.** What is the approximate total timeline from lot acquisition to building-ready status?

**Q6.** Which of the following is NOT part of the underground infrastructure that Land Development is responsible for?

### Section B: Process & Decision-Making (Open Response)

**Q11.** Walk us through how you actually evaluate a potential lot when a client brings you an option on a piece of land. Beyond the checklist items (zoning, flood zone, utilities), what are the first things you look at physically on the property and what red flags would make you walk away?

**Q12.** Describe the real relationship between your department and the civil engineers you contract. How do you select an engineer for a new project? Do you have preferred engineers, and what makes one better than another for different types of sites?

**Q13.** When you are coordinating site work and the subcontractor encounters unexpected conditions underground (rock, old utilities, water table issues), walk us through the decision-making process. Who do you call, how fast do costs escalate, and at what point do you stop work versus push through?

**Q14.** Explain the practical differences between developing a lot with municipal sewer versus one that requires a septic system. How does this affect your timeline, cost, permitting, and which county departments get involved?

**Q15.** Describe your process for estimating infrastructure costs on a new lot before committing to purchase. What are the biggest cost variables that can swing a deal from profitable to not worth it? Give us specific dollar ranges for common items.

**Q16.** Walk us through a typical day when you have active site work happening. How do you monitor the sub's progress? How often are you physically on site versus managing remotely? What communication tools actually work best for field coordination?

**Q17.** When a compact test fails, describe everything that happens next. How common is this, what typically causes it, how much does it delay the schedule, and what does the rework process look like?

**Q18.** Explain how you coordinate with the Permits department during the transition from land development to building phase. What documents do you prepare, what information is critical, and what is the most common thing that gets missed in the handoff?

**Q19.** Describe how you handle lots that have wetlands issues. What is the actual process for dealing with the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE)? How long does a wetlands determination take, and what are the mitigation options?

**Q20.** How do you maintain your list of subcontractors for site work? What criteria do you use to evaluate them, how do you handle pricing negotiations, and what happens when your preferred sub is booked for months?

### Section C: Advanced Scenarios & Edge Cases (Open Response)

**Q21.** You are evaluating a lot in a new area of Marion County. The zoning says R-1 residential, but the lot is adjacent to an agricultural property with active livestock. The title search reveals an easement for a drainage ditch that runs through the back third of the property. The FEMA map shows the lot is partially in a Special Flood Hazard Area. Walk through your complete analysis: would you recommend proceeding, and under what conditions?

**Q22.** A client purchases a lot and you begin due diligence. The gopher tortoise survey finds 3 active burrows. The FWC permit process will add 90 days to the timeline. The client is impatient and asks if there is any way to speed this up or work around it. What do you tell them, what are the legal risks, and how do you manage the client's expectations?

**Q23.** During site work on a 5-lot subdivision, your earthwork sub discovers what appears to be an old septic system from a previous structure that was never properly decommissioned. Describe every step you take, every authority you notify, and the impact on your timeline and budget.

**Q24.** You have two lots under evaluation simultaneously. Lot A is cheaper, has municipal water and sewer, but is in a flood zone requiring elevation. Lot B is more expensive, requires septic and well, but is on high ground with no flood concerns. Build out the complete cost comparison you would present to the client, including hidden costs that most people overlook.

**Q25.** A lot in Citrus County has been in the permitting process for 10 weeks (longer than the typical 4-6 weeks). The county keeps asking for additional documentation -- first an updated environmental study, then a traffic impact analysis, now a noise study. Describe how you determine whether this is normal county process or whether something else is going on (political opposition, zoning issues, etc.), and what you do about it.

**Q26.** Your site work sub is halfway through underground utility installation when the county issues a stop-work order because a neighbor filed a complaint about stormwater runoff onto their property. Describe how you handle this: the immediate response, the county negotiation, the engineering fix, and how you get the project moving again.

**Q27.** Describe the biggest surprise cost you have encountered on a land development project -- something that was not caught during due diligence. What went wrong, how much did it cost, and what process change did you implement to prevent it from happening again?

**Q28.** When transitioning a lot from Land Development to Building, you need to provide compaction test results, utility connection confirmations, and permit numbers. But what about the informal knowledge -- the things about a lot that do not show up in any document? What verbal warnings or notes do you pass along to the Scheduling and Supervisao teams about specific lot conditions?

**Q29.** You are developing infrastructure for a small community (10 lots) and need to coordinate road construction, underground utilities, and drainage for the entire subdivision simultaneously. Describe the sequencing -- what must happen first, what can be done in parallel, and what are the coordination points where one sub's work blocks another's.

**Q30.** If Lakeshore were to adopt the concessionaire model (51/49) and a new operator in a different part of Florida needed to replicate your land development process, what are the three things that are hardest to standardize? What knowledge lives only in your head or your personal relationships that would be the most difficult to transfer?
